# Proposal Draft

## Overview

Discovery notes + price list → a proposal the client can say yes to without a second meeting. The hard rule: every number comes from the user's pricing input, and every promise from what the user actually offered — the draft persuades by clarity, not by inflation.

## When to Use

- Service proposals, project quotes, retainer offers, agency/freelance pitches after a discovery call
- NOT for: legal contract drafting (terms here are a summary; the contract is a lawyer's document), or proposals with no discovery input at all (ask three questions first — see step 1)

## Process

1. **Check the inputs.** Needed: (a) what the client said they need — call notes, transcript (meeting-notes skill output plugs in directly), email thread; (b) what the user sells and their prices/rates; (c) constraints — deadline, budget signals, decision process. Missing (a) or (b) → ask for the minimum: client's stated problem, offered solution, price basis. **Never fill pricing gaps yourself.** Unknown price → `[PRICE: your call]` marker.
2. **Frame the problem in the client's own words.** Open the proposal with their situation and cost-of-doing-nothing as *they* described it — quotes from discovery beat marketing prose. A client who sees themselves in paragraph one keeps reading.
3. **Scope the offer.** What's included as concrete deliverables (nouns and numbers: "12 posts/month", "one landing page, two revision rounds"), what's explicitly excluded (the line that prevents scope-fight later), assumptions the price rests on, and client-side prerequisites (access, content, decisions).
4. **Price it.** Default to 2–3 options when the user's pricing supports it (good/better/best anchoring — recommend the middle one and say why); one option when they sell one thing. Each option: price, what changes vs. the tier below, payment schedule from the user's terms.
5. **Timeline and terms:** phases with durations (start date as a dependency on signing, not a fantasy date), validity window for the offer, revision policy, and the user's standard terms summarized — flagged as "summary, full terms in contract".
6. **Close with one next step** — a single concrete action ("reply 'option B' and I'll send the invoice + kickoff slots"), not "looking forward to hearing from you".
7. **Deliver** as clean Markdown (export-ready), in the client's language, ≤2 pages. All `[PRICE]`/`[YOUR CALL]` markers listed at the top for the user to resolve before sending.

## Verification

- [ ] Zero invented numbers: every price traces to the user's input or is a marked gap
- [ ] Problem section uses the client's stated words/facts, not generic pain prose
- [ ] Exclusions and assumptions present — scope has edges
- [ ] Exactly one next-step CTA; unresolved markers surfaced at the top
